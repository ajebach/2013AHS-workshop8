Workshop 8 - Chi-Square Tests: Analysing Categorical Data

Research Methods and Statistics - T2 2025

Introduction

Welcome to Workshop 8: Chi-Square Tests. Building on your hypothesis testing knowledge, today we’ll explore how to analyse relationships between categorical variables, essential for yes/no outcomes, group memberships, and frequency counts.

This session guides you through conducting chi-square tests and introduces a new concept: how to identify which specific cells drive significant results (similar to post-hoc tests in ANOVA, but for categorical data).

Learning Objectives

  • Conduct and interpret chi-square tests of independence for two categorical variables
  • Apply chi-square goodness of fit tests for single categorical variables
  • Learn to identify which cells contribute to significant results
  • Create visualisations for categorical data
  • Communicate findings with appropriate clinical context

What is Chi-Square Testing Good For?

Chi-square (χ²) tests analyse categorical data—data that falls into distinct groups rather than continuous measurements. In exercise science, we use it to examine injury patterns, training adherence, or response categories. In pharmacy, it’s essential for analysing adverse event frequencies, prescription patterns, or treatment compliance rates.

Real-world example: When comparing whether different exercise programmes (resistance, aerobic, combined) lead to different adherence rates (adhered, dropped out), chi-square tests whether the distribution differs from what we’d expect by chance. If 40% typically drop out overall, do all programmes have 40% dropout, or does it vary by programme type?

Key Terms Review:

  • χ² statistic: Measures how much observed frequencies deviate from expected frequencies
  • Expected frequencies: What we’d expect if variables were independent
  • Degrees of freedom: df = (rows-1) × (columns-1)
  • p-value: Probability of observing this pattern (or more extreme) if no real association exists

Section C: Understanding Post-Hoc Analysis with Standardised Residuals

When your chi-square test is significant, you know there’s an association between variables, but which specific groups differ? This is where we need a technique similar to post-hoc tests in ANOVA.

NEW CONCEPT: Standardised Residuals

What are they? Standardised residuals tell us how much each cell deviates from what we’d expect if the variables were independent. Think of them sort of like z-scores for each cell in your contingency table.

How to interpret them:

  • Greater than +2: This cell has significantly MORE cases than expected
  • Less than -2: This cell has significantly FEWER cases than expected
  • Between -2 and +2: This cell is about what we’d expect

Example: If Lions Goal has a standardised residual of +2.5, this means Brisbane scores significantly more goals than we’d expect if team and scoring outcome were unrelated.

This is your post-hoc analysis for chi-square, it shows you WHERE the significant association is occurring!

Summary and Key Takeaways

Based on your analyses, you should now understand:

Chi-Square Test of Homogeneity: Compares whether different populations/groups have the same distribution for a categorical variable. Tests if separate groups show similar patterns. Example we did: Do Brisbane Lions and Geelong Cats have the same distribution of scoring outcomes?

Chi-Square Goodness of Fit: Compares observed frequencies in a single sample to expected/theoretical distributions. Tests whether your sample follows population norms or a hypothesized pattern. Example we did: Does our pharmacy’s adherence pattern (actual) match industry standards (expected 50%/30%/20%)?

Chi-Square Test of Independence: Examines whether two categorical variables are related within a single population. Tests if knowing one variable helps predict the other. Example er did: Is exercise type associated with injury status among gym members?

Standardised Residuals: Your key tool for understanding WHERE differences occur in chi-square tests. Values > |2| indicate cells that deviate significantly from what we’d expect if there was no association.

Practical/Clinical Context: A statistically significant chi-square doesn’t always mean clinically important differences. Consider absolute percentage differences and practical implications.

Remember: Chi-square tells us IF variables are associated. Standardised residuals tell us WHERE the association occurs. Practical/clinical judgment determines IF IT MATTERS in practice.
